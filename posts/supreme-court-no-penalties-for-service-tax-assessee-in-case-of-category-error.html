Supreme Court: No Penalties for Service Tax Assessee in Case of Category Error

In a significant ruling, a Division Bench of the Supreme Court, comprising Justices Abhay S. Oka and Sanjay Karol, made a pivotal determination during a recent hearing of appeals. The core of their judgment centers on the principle that an "assessee cannot be subjected to a penalty on the basis of a show cause notice containing a completely erroneous category of service."

As a consequence of this ruling, the Court declared the demand made based on the aforementioned show cause notice to be illegal. The appeals in question stemmed from service tax demands rooted in four Show Cause Notices, all issued under Section 73 of the Finance Act, 1994. These notices pertained to the demand of service tax and are detailed as follows:

  • Show-cause notice dated: 19/10/2009 (Issued for the period of 1.4.2004 to 31.3.2009)
  • Show-cause notice dated: 20/10/2010 (Issued for the period of 1.4.2009 to 31.3.2010)
  • Show-cause notice dated: 21/10/2011 (Issued for the period of 1.4.2010 to 31.3.2011)
  • Show-cause notice dated: 22/10/2012 (Issued for the period of 1.4.2011 to 31.3.2012)

To provide context, the impugned judgment, dated 18th September 2018, by the Customs, Excise and Service Tax Appellate Tribunal (CESTAT) held that the services in dispute fell into the category of "Information Technology Software" starting from 16th May 2008. Prior to that date, the same services were classified as "Intellectual Property Service." Importantly, the Tribunal also found the show-cause notice dated 19th October 2019, which covered the period up to 16th May 2008, to be unjustified.

Subsequently, the Court addressed the appeal filed by the revenue, which was limited to the first show cause notice. This notice pertained to taxable service under the label of "Management, Maintenance, and Repair." The Court noted that the demand was associated with services provided by the assessee regarding third-party software, in-house developed software, or customized software. These services involved the temporary transfer of the right to use said software to clients. Consequently, the correct categorization prior to 16th May 2008 was "Intellectual Property Service," and from 16th May 2008 onwards, it was "Information Technology Software."

In light of these observations, the Court deemed the classification in the first show cause notice as entirely erroneous and refrained from intervening in the CESTAT's findings. It stressed that "elementary principles of natural justice required that the adjudication on the basis of show-cause notice should be made only on the basis of classification stated in the show cause notice. Assessee cannot be subjected to a penalty on the basis of a show cause notice containing a completely erroneous category of service."

Moving forward, the Court proceeded to address the appeal filed by the assessee. In this phase, the Court thoroughly examined the validity of the other three show cause notices. Upon close scrutiny, it found that the other three notices correctly identified the classification, and their conclusions were meticulously based on a comprehensive analysis of both factual and legal aspects. Consequently, the Court declined to interfere and dismissed the appeal.
